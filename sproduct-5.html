Microscope

produces magnified visual images of objects too small to be seen with the naked eye

A microscope is an instrument used to magnify small objects that are too tiny to be seen with the naked eye. It works by using a system of lenses—objective lenses near the specimen and an eyepiece—to bend light rays and enlarge the image. The two main types are optical (light) microscopes, which use visible light and glass lenses for viewing cells and tissues up to ~1000x, and electron microscopes, which use beams of electrons to achieve much higher magnification (up to millions of x) for viewing ultra-fine details like viruses or protein structures. Key specifications include resolution (the ability to distinguish two close points), magnification power, and numerical aperture. For lab use, you also have inverted microscopes for viewing live cells in culture dishes and fluorescence microscopes that use specific wavelengths to tag and visualize particular molecules.
